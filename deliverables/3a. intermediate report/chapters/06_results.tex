\section{Preliminary Results}

The preliminary results of this project consist of an exploratory data analysis conducted across all three data sources: the Baumkataster, the Sentinel-2 satellite imagery and the OpensStreetMap (OSM) urban context layers. The goal of the analysis was to asses data quality, learnnfeature distributions and identify which indicators are likely to be useful for training the U-Net model.

\subsection{Baumkataster Analysis}

This section contains some analysis on the Baumkataster data sets.

\subsubsection{Dataset Overview and Tree Counts}

The cleaned Baumkataster data contain 226,998 trees for Vienna, 217,454 for Paris, 233,505 for Hamburg and 43,367 for Barcelona, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:tree_counts}. Vienna, Paris, and Hamburg are roughly balanced in size and are suitable as training cities. However, Barcelona lacks size attributes and a geometry column and  therefore it will only be used as point locations for cross-city generalization testing.

\subsubsection{Tree Size Distributions}

The available size attributes differ across cities. Vienna provides height categories, trunk circumference, and crown diameter. Paris provides height and circumference. Hamburg provides circumference and crown diameter.\\
\\
Figure~\ref{fig:size_histogram} shows the distributions of height, circumference, and crown diameter across cities. The histograms reveal two data-definition issues specific to Vienna. First, the \texttt{height} column stores ordinal category codes from 1 to 8, representing height ranges from 0--5\,m up to \textgreater35\,m, rather than continuous values in meters. This is confirmed by the raw mean of 2.21, which is not interpretable as a physical mean height in metres, but as the average of category codes. Second, the Vienna \texttt{crown\_diameter} variable is also stored as ordinal category codes linked to crown-diameter intervals, and is therefore not directly comparable to continuous crown-diameter measurements from other cities. By contrast, Paris height values are stored in metres and and show a realistic distribution with a median around 8\,m. The planned fix is to decode the Vienna height and crown-diameter classes to approximate interval midpoints before model training.

    (capped at 99th percentile for readability).}

\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Species Composition}

Species diversity is high across all cities, with 670 unique species in Vienna, 996 in Paris, 354 in Hamburg, and 330 in Barcelona. This reflects different urban planting contexts and climates. Across all four cities, 86 species are shared, predominantly from the \textit{Abies} and \textit{Acer} genera. Species identity will not be used as a direct model input, but the diversity and climate-specific composition inform expectations about canopy shape and size variation across cities. Figure~\ref{fig:species} shows the ten most common species in each city. Vienna is dominated by \textit{Acer platanoides} (Norway maple, 19,244 trees) and \textit{Aesculus hippocastanum} (horse chestnut, 11,661 trees), both classic central European street tree species. Paris is dominated by \textit{Platanus x hispanica} (London plane, 38,113 trees). Hamburg is dominated by \textit{Quercus robur} (English oak, 42,965 trees) and \textit{Tilia x europaea} (European lime, 33,852 trees). Barcelona shows a different composition with subtropical species such as \textit{Tipuana tipu} and \textit{Pinus pinea}, reflecting its Mediterranean climate.

Figure~\ref{fig:spatial_scatter} shows the spatial scatter of labeled tree locations for each city. The scatter plots indicate that tree locations are distributed unevenly across urban space rather than uniformly across each study area. This is relevant for downstream model development because raw patch sampling would otherwise overrepresent dense clusters and underrepresent sparser parts of the cities.

\subsubsection{Street Trees vs. Park Trees}

A spatial cross-check between the Baumkataster and OSM landuse polygons revealed a strong difference in tree placement patterns across cities, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:green_zones}. Importantly, these percentages were computed on sampled trees rather than on the full tree population. In the sampled data, 52.3\% of Vienna trees, 42.2\% of Paris trees, and 2.6\% of Hamburg trees fall inside green landuse zones. This suggests that Hamburg is much more strongly characterized by street-planted trees, whereas Vienna and Paris contain a substantially larger share of trees inside parks and other green landuse classes. For training, this means the model cannot rely on green space alone as a proxy for tree presence.

\subsubsection{EVI and SAVI Scaling}

Another data quality issue was identified within the EVI and SAVI indices. EVI values exceed 1.0 for all cities in summer, which is false as EVI is bounded between $-1$ and $+1$. This is a systematic issue across all cities. The same issue could affects SAVI, although it is not clearly visible in the results, where SAVI values remain within the bounds. The root cause of this is that the index the formulas were applied to the raw pixel values stored in the satellite files, which are large integers, instead of the actual light reflectance values they represent. Converting raw values to reflectance requires dividing by 10,000. NDVI is unaffected here because its formula divides two numbers, so the scale cancels out either way. ince the raw bands are preserved in the TIF stacks, no rescraping is required. The planned fix is to divide the raw band values by 10,000 before recomputing EVI and SAVI. Until this is done, only NDVI will be used as a vegetation index input channel.

\subsection{OSM Analysis}

The OSM layers were loaded for all four cities across four feature types: roads, buildings, landuse, and water. Road networks are dominated by \texttt{footway}, \texttt{service}, and \texttt{residential} classes across all cities. Hamburg's OSM extract is noticeably sparser than the other cities, with 24,184 road features compared to 161,378 for Vienna, which will be investigated before using Hamburg as a training city.\\
\\
Building footprints are present for all cities and will serve as reliable negative labels during training mask generation, since no trees are expected on rooftop surfaces. Road classes such as \texttt{footway}, \texttt{path}, \texttt{steps} and \texttt{track} will not be used as hard negative masks, because these routes can pass through parks alongside trees and would introduce false negatives into the training labels.\\

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{images/fig_12.png}
    \caption{Road type distribution per city as a percentage of total road features.}
    \label{fig:roads}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:roads} shows the road type composition per city. Footway is the largest road class in Barcelona, Paris, and Vienna, but not in Hamburg, where \texttt{service} roads are more frequent than \texttt{footway}. Only wider road classes such as \texttt{primary}, \texttt{secondary}, and \texttt{motorway} are more suitable candidates for reliable tree-free zones.

\subsubsection{Landuse Distribution}

Figure~\ref{fig:landuse} shows the landuse distribution by area for each city. Vienna and Paris contain large mapped green areas, while Hamburg’s OSM layers are substantially sparser than those of Vienna and Paris. While part of this difference may reflect a smaller study area, the discrepancy is large relative to Hamburg’s comparable tree-label count, so OSM coverage and preprocessing consistency should be checked before using Hamburg heavily as a training city.
